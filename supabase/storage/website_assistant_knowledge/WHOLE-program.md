# Whole — Program Knowledge Base (Condensed)

**Program:** Whole — A 16-week coaching-based group program integrating positive psychology, exercise science, nutrition, and communication skills
**Creator:** Scott Crawford — Associate Certified Coach (ICF) & Certified Personal Trainer (ISSA)
**Philosophy:** "Do What You Can" — an adaptive, self-compassionate approach that meets participants where they are and empowers gradual mastery, flexibility, and resilience over perfectionism.

> *"The promise of this program is not 'we'll give you a plan.' It's 'we'll help you understand yourself well enough that the right plan becomes obvious.'"*

---

## The Open → Deepen → Move Coaching Flow

The Whole program teaches participants a simple, three-move conversational rhythm that governs all peer coaching interactions. This flow emerged naturally from the program's foundational principles — it is not a separate model layered on top, but a recognition of the pattern those principles already create when used in conversation.

### The Three Moves

**Open** — Create space. Let the person talk. Find out what's alive for them right now.

**Deepen** — Go beneath the surface. Explore what's really driving the presenting topic. Resist the urge to solve.

**Move** — Help the person find one honest, right-sized step forward. Not your step. Theirs.

### How the Foundational Principles Power Each Move

| Move | What It Does | Foundational Principle at Work | Starter Questions |
|------|-------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| **Open** | Creates space, invites the topic, builds safety | Three Prerequisites — listening for which one brought this person to the conversation (dissatisfaction, vision, or willingness) | "What's on your mind?" · "What's important today?" · "What's been occupying you?" |
| **Deepen** | Explores what's underneath, challenges assumptions gently, holds silence | Three Coaching Questions — "What do you want? What do you *really* want? What are you prepared to give up?" · First Principles: "What are we here to do?" | "What's really driving that?" · "And what else?" · "What would that give you that you don't have now?" · "What are we actually trying to achieve here?" |
| **Move** | Finds a right-sized next step anchored to the person's reality | Goal-Setting Cascade — right-sizing to current capacity · "Do What You Can" philosophy | "What's one small thing you could try?" · "Is this the version you can actually do this week?" · "What would you do first?" |

### The Coaching Stance: Stay Curious

The three moves describe what to do. The coaching stance describes how to be while doing it. Michael Bungay Stanier's insight captures it simply: **stay curious a little longer, rush to advice a little slower.** This is the discipline that makes Open → Deepen → Move work — particularly the Deepen move, where the temptation to fix, solve, or advise is strongest.

Bungay Stanier's "And what else?" is the single most useful peer coaching question in the program. It prevents the most common mistake: latching onto the first thing someone says and trying to fix it. It belongs in the Deepen move and should be used generously.

**"Do What You Can" governs how participants treat themselves. "Stay Curious" governs how they treat each other.**

### How the Flow Evolves Across the Program

Open → Deepen → Move is introduced formally in Week 5 and practised explicitly through triads from that point forward. But the moves are present implicitly from Week 1 — participants have been experiencing them through the facilitator's modelling before they learn to name them.

The three moves stay the same across all 16 weeks. What changes is the depth participants bring to each move as new knowledge accumulates:

| Phase | What Deepens |
|-------|-------------|
| **Foundation (Weeks 1–5)** | Deepen uses the Three Coaching Questions and First Principles directly. Move produces micro-wins. The flow is scaffolded and conscious. |
| **Communication (Week 6)** | Deepen gains emotional granularity — participants can now name the feeling behind the reaction and the need behind the feeling. |
| **The Body (Weeks 7–9)** | Deepen distinguishes health goals from performance goals with real understanding. Move applies the Infinite/Finite distinction to physical training. |
| **Fuel & Recovery (Weeks 10–14)** | Deepen becomes powerful around setbacks (Week 11) — "What do you believe about yourself right now, and is that belief serving you?" Move includes the ability to release a goal without shame. Nutritional content adds new domains for the flow to operate in. |
| **Integration (Weeks 15–16)** | The full flow is instinctive. Deepen draws on values, identity, and the full arc of the program. Move connects to identity-based goals and the self-coaching loop. Participants own the flow and can use it independently — with others and with themselves. |

### The Self-Coaching Version

By the integration phase, participants learn to run Open → Deepen → Move on themselves:

- **Open:** What's happening? What am I noticing? (Observation without judgment)
- **Deepen:** What's really going on here? What do I actually want? What's the story I'm telling myself? (The Three Coaching Questions turned inward)
- **Move:** What's the smallest honest step I can take from where I actually am? (The "Do What You Can" principle applied to self-direction)

This solo version connects directly to the self-coaching loop formalised in Weeks 15–16: **Observe → Reflect → Adjust → Recommit** — which is Open → Deepen → Move expressed as an ongoing rhythm rather than a single conversation.

### Beyond the Program

Open → Deepen → Move is not limited to peer coaching triads. It is a conversational habit that transfers to any context where a better outcome is possible: conversations with a partner, a colleague, a teenager, or oneself. The three moves work because they follow the natural architecture of a productive conversation — and because they are powered by principles (the coaching questions, first principles, right-sizing) that apply universally.

The program teaches this flow in a coaching context. Participants take it with them as a life skill.

## Goal-Setting Philosophy — Tactical Finite Goals Within an Infinite Direction

The Whole program treats goal-setting not as a standalone skill to be taught and completed, but as a living practice woven throughout every session. The foundational insight: most goal-setting frameworks — including SMART — are tactical tools. They shape goals effectively, but they cannot tell you whether the goal is the right one, whether it matters to you, or whether the cost of pursuing it is one you're willing to pay.

Before any goal is shaped, it must be understood. We do the thinking before the planning.

### Infinite Goals and Finite Goals

Every goal a participant sets falls into one of two categories, and identifying which category comes first — before any planning, shaping, or commitment.

| **Infinite Goal (Strategy)** | **Finite Goal (Tactics)** |
|------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Health goal — ongoing, identity-based | Performance goal — bounded, measurable |
| *"Become someone who moves consistently"* | *"Run 5K in under 30 minutes by June"* |
| No finish line; expressed as rituals and rhythms | Clear endpoint; expressed as targets and deadlines |
| Sustained by values and identity | Sustained by motivation and accountability |
| Resilient to disruption — the direction persists | Vulnerable to disruption — a missed target feels like failure |

Most participants will hold both types simultaneously. The infinite goal is the compass; the finite goals are the steps. This is the hybrid model at the heart of the program:

> *Tactical finite goals within the confines of an infinite goal.*

The finite goals serve the infinite direction. When a finite goal stops serving the infinite anchor — because life changed, because the participant learned something new, because their values shifted — it is retired without guilt and replaced. The strategy endures; the tactics rotate.

### The Limitations of SMART

SMART is repositioned in this program: not as step one, but as step three or four. A tactical tool deployed after the strategic thinking is done.

SMART assumes you already know what you want. It has no mechanism for discovering whether the goal is genuinely yours. It is silent on cost — it asks whether a goal is achievable, but not "What are you prepared to give up?" It favours finite goals by design. It does not account for readiness.

> *"You've probably met SMART before. It's a good tool — but it's a shaping tool, not a thinking tool. If you use it before you've done the thinking, you end up with a very well-formatted goal that might not be yours. We're going to do the thinking first."*

### The Goal-Setting Cascade

The following sequence is the operational model for goal-setting throughout the program:

**Step 1 — What Game Are You Playing?** Identify the goal type: Infinite (Health/Strategy) or Finite (Performance/Tactics). This frames everything that follows.

**Step 2 — What's Underneath It?** Apply the Three Coaching Questions: What do I want? What do I really want? What am I prepared to give up? This is the thinking before the planning — the element most programs skip.

**Step 3 — Shape It.** If finite: apply SMART. If infinite: define rituals, rhythms, and a review cadence. If both (the most common scenario): keep the infinite anchor visible and choose a right-sized finite step that serves it. Always right-sized to current readiness using the Transtheoretical Model as a filter. "Is this the version of this goal I can actually do this week?"

**Step 4 — Protect Autonomy.** Self-Determination Theory and ICF coaching ethics converge. Autonomy is preserved because the participant decides the goal type. Competence is protected because the goal is right-sized. Relatedness is built through the peer coaching around it.

**Step 5 — Revisit as New Information Arrives.** Each new module is an invitation to add, refine, or replace a goal. A failed tactic is not a failed strategy.

### The Setback Reframe

> *"A failed tactic isn't a failed strategy."*

Missing a finite goal — three walks this week, a nutrition target, a sleep commitment — does not mean the infinite direction is broken. It means that particular tactic didn't survive contact with that particular week. The response is not self-criticism. The response is: pick a new tactic. The strategy holds.

### Where Goal-Setting Appears

Goal-setting does not occupy its own dedicated session. The concepts are taught in a compressed block within an early Foundation-phase session (15–20 minutes of direct teaching), and the practice is distributed across the entire 16-week program. This sends the message that goal-setting is not an event but an operating system.

| Phase | Weeks | Goal-Setting Integration |
|-------|-------|--------------------------|
| **Foundation** | 1–4 | Infinite/Finite distinction and SMART limitations taught in compressed block. Participants set absurdly small micro-wins — "too small to fail." Three Coaching Questions introduced as the lens through which goals are examined. |
| **Communication** | 5–6 | Peer coaching skills applied to goal support via Open → Deepen → Move. Participants practise asking each other the Three Coaching Questions. First structured goal commitment (movement focus). |
| **The Body** | 7–9 | New knowledge on strength, cardio, and recovery becomes an invitation to add or refine movement goals. Finite tactics may be revised in light of new understanding. Open → Deepen → Move applied to physical goals in triads. |
| **Fuel & Recovery** | 10–14 | Nutrition and sleep content invites new goal domains. Mid-course revision checkpoint: "Does this finite step still serve my infinite direction, given what I now know?" Open → Deepen → Move applied to setbacks and recovery. |
| **Integration** | 15–16 | Values integration: shift remaining goals from outcome to process or values-based framing. Final revision. Build self-coaching loop. Foundational principles, goal-setting cascade, and Open → Deepen → Move handed over as permanent tools. |

## Program Overview

Whole is a 16-week group coaching and education program that integrates positive psychology, behavioural science, exercise physiology, nutrition, and coaching communication into one cohesive learning journey.

The guiding philosophy is **"Do what you can"** — an adaptive, self-compassionate approach that meets participants where they are and empowers gradual mastery, flexibility, and resilience over perfectionism.

Participants learn not only to improve their own wellbeing, but to coach one another through the process — developing communication, empathy, and leadership skills that transfer directly to personal and professional relationships. The Open → Deepen → Move coaching flow is the conversational engine that makes this peer coaching effective and transferable beyond the program.

## Target Participant Profile

Whole is designed for adults (typically 30–60) who want to build sustainable habits around movement, nutrition, and mindset — but have found prescriptive programs difficult to maintain. They may be navigating career transitions, midlife health shifts, burnout, or simply wanting more from their daily routines. They are curious, willing to reflect, and open to learning alongside others. They don't need to be athletes or have coaching experience — just a willingness to try.

## Progressive Goal Architecture

The goal architecture follows the program's core insight: *tactical finite goals within the confines of an infinite goal.* Goals start absurdly small — too small to fail — and evolve as participants develop self-awareness, knowledge, and coaching skill. At every stage, the infinite direction (the health goal, the compass) remains visible. Finite tactics rotate and adapt. A failed tactic is never a failed strategy.

| Phase | Goal Approach | Goal-Setting Cascade in Action | Example |
|-------|---------------|-------------------------------|---------|
| Weeks 1–4: Micro-Wins | Absurdly small actions to guarantee success and build self-efficacy. Infinite/Finite distinction taught. | Step 1 (What game?) and Step 2 (What's underneath?) are the focus. No shaping yet — just thinking. | "Walk 5 min after dinner twice this week." |
| Week 5: First Commitment | Formalise one goal from proven micro-wins. SMART applied for the first time — as Step 3, after the thinking is done. Open → Deepen → Move practised formally for the first time around goals. | Full cascade practised: What game → What's underneath → Shape it → Protect autonomy. | "Walk/run 3× per week + stretch before bed." |
| Weeks 8–10: Adaptive Growth | Refine goals with new knowledge from strength, aerobic, and recovery modules. Finite tactics may be revised or replaced. | Step 5: Revisit as new information arrives. "Does this tactic still serve my infinite direction?" | "Add 2 bodyweight sessions + track aerobic walks at comfortable HR." |
| Weeks 13–14: Values Integration | Connect goals to identity and values. Shift from outcome to process. The infinite goal becomes explicit and owned. | The infinite anchor is named: "I'm becoming someone who..." Finite goals are checked against it. | "I'm becoming someone who nourishes their body." |
| Weeks 15–16: Maintenance | Build a lifelong self-coaching loop: Observe → Reflect → Adjust → Recommit. The goal-setting cascade and Open → Deepen → Move become permanent tools. | Participants own the full cascade and the coaching flow. They can run both independently on any future goal or conversation. | Personal maintenance plan and manifesto. |

## Week-by-Week Calendar

### Pre-Course: Welcome Pack & Self-Assessment

**Theme:** Know Your Foundation

**Summary:** Participants complete the VIA Character Strengths survey, an optional Optimism baseline test (ISSA), and receive a one-page PERMA overview introducing the five dimensions of wellbeing. The PERMA sheet includes a brief introduction to Kaufman's sailboat metaphor — a hull of security, connection, and self-esteem that keeps you afloat, and a sail of exploration, love, and purpose that moves you forward. This plants the seed that growth is non-linear and ongoing, not a ladder to climb. Participants bring their top 5 strengths to Week 1 as icebreaker material.

**Key Content:** VIA survey · Optimism scoring (optional) · PERMA introduction sheet with Kaufman sailboat

**Home Task:** Complete assessments, reflect on results, bring findings to first session.

**Sources:** Seligman (PERMA, Learned Optimism) · VIA Institute · ISSA Transformation Specialist · Kaufman (Transcend — sailboat metaphor)

---

### Week 1 — Orientation & Foundations

**Theme:** What It Means to Be Whole
**Phase:** Foundation

**Summary:** Welcome, group agreements (confidentiality, curiosity, compassion), and program overview. Introduce the "Do What You Can" philosophy. Coaching vs. training vs. therapy — defining scope. Icebreaker: share one VIA strength and a story behind it. **Live Core Values card sort** — participants explore and choose their top values as a group activity, generating immediate connection and discussion. Introduce PERMA as a lens for wellbeing (not a scorecard).

**Foundational Principles Introduction:** This is where the philosophical backbone of the program is explicitly taught. The facilitator introduces the **Three Prerequisites for Successful Coaching** — dissatisfaction, vision, and willingness — and frames them as conditions that apply whether you're being coached, coaching someone else, or coaching yourself. Participants are invited to consider: *Which of these three brought me here? Is my dissatisfaction genuine? Do I have a vision, even a fuzzy one? Am I willing — and what might that willingness cost me?*

The **Three Coaching Questions** are introduced as a practical translation: What do I want? What do I *really* want? What am I prepared to give up?

The **First Principles approach** is introduced with the orienting question: *"What are we here to do?"* The facilitator frames this as the question that cuts through noise, stories, excuses, and old patterns — the question participants will return to whenever they feel stuck, overwhelmed, or off track throughout the program.

The tone is warm and invitational, not confrontational. These are seeds being planted. Participants don't need to have all the answers today — they need to know these questions exist and that the program will keep returning to them.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Implicit modelling:** The facilitator models the coaching flow throughout this session without naming it. The icebreaker is an Open move (creating space for stories). The values card sort and the foundational principles are Deepen moves (exploring what matters underneath). The home task is a Move (one small step forward). Participants are experiencing the rhythm before they learn its name.

**Key Content:** Program structure & rhythm · "Do What You Can" ethos · ICF ethics & scope · PERMA as a meaning framework · Group agreements · Core Values card sort (live) · **Three Prerequisites for Coaching** · **Three Coaching Questions** · **First Principles: "What are we here to do?"** · Kaufman (growth as non-linear, whole-person development)

**Breakout:** "What brought me here?" — share motivations and hopes in small groups. Begin connecting values to motivations. Facilitator invites participants to notice which of the Three Prerequisites feels strongest for them right now.

**Home Task:** Watch Brené Brown's "Power of Vulnerability" TED Talk. Journal: "What brought me here, and what do I hope will be different? Which of the three coaching questions — what do I want, what do I really want, what am I prepared to give up — feels most alive for me right now?"

**Coaching Skill:** Establishing psychological safety and confidentiality

**Goal Action:** No formal goals yet — observe and settle in. The goal-setting philosophy has not yet been taught. Participants may notice aspirations forming; the instruction is simply to notice, not to act.

**Sources:** ICF Code of Ethics · Brown (Dare to Lead) · Seligman (PERMA) · VIA Institute · Kaufman (Transcend)

### Week 2 — Motivation & Meaning

**Theme:** What Drives Us to Act
**Phase:** Foundation

**Summary:** Explore intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation, autonomy, mastery, and purpose (Self-Determination Theory). Connect values and strengths to motivation — "When I act from what matters, effort feels different." Introduce the concept that wellbeing includes meaning, not just happiness. PERMA's "M" — meaningful effort is valuable even when uncomfortable.

**Foundational Principles Development:** This week deepens the first coaching question — *What do I want?* — by distinguishing between surface motivations and deeper drivers. The SDT framework helps participants see that what they think they want (a body shape, a number on a scale, a promotion) may not be what they *really* want (autonomy, mastery, connection, meaning). Kaufman's framing of self-actualisation as exploration rather than achievement complements this — growth isn't about arriving somewhere; it's about how you travel.

The second coaching question — *What do I really want?* — is explicitly revisited. Participants begin to notice the gap between presenting desires and underlying needs. This is the beginning of genuine self-coaching.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Implicit modelling continues:** The breakout prompt ("Where do I feel stuck right now?") is an Open move. The partner's invitation to ask "What do you think is really underneath that?" is a Deepen move — and it mirrors the Three Coaching Questions directly. The micro-win experiment is a Move. Participants are practising the rhythm without the label.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** SDT directly informs the goal-setting cascade's Step 4 (Protect Autonomy). When motivation feels forced, it's usually because one of SDT's three needs is unmet — autonomy, competence, or relatedness. This week plants the understanding that will make Step 4 feel intuitive when the cascade is formally taught in Week 3.

The distinction between surface and deeper motivations is the same work that the cascade's Step 2 (What's Underneath It?) formalises. Participants are already doing the thinking that makes goal-setting meaningful — they just don't have the framework yet.

**Key Content:** Self-Determination Theory · Intrinsic/extrinsic motivation · Strengths as motivational fuel · Meaning vs. happiness reframe · Kaufman (self-actualisation as exploration) · Grant (progress over perfection)

**Breakout:** "Where do I feel stuck right now?" — peer pairs explore using open questions only. Encourage partners to ask: "What do you think is really underneath that?"

**Home Task:** Journal: "When have I felt most alive and why? What strength was I using? And — what do I *really* want from this program?"

**Coaching Skill:** Asking open questions — practise curiosity over advice

**Goal Action:** Invite one tiny "micro-win" experiment (e.g., drink water before coffee). Too small to fail. No framework yet — just a first step. Facilitator language: *"Pick something so small you're certain you'll succeed. We're not planning yet — we're experimenting."*

**Sources:** Deci & Ryan (SDT) · Seligman · Dweck (Mindset) · ISSA behavior change · Kaufman (Transcend) · Grant (Hidden Potential)

### Week 3 — Habits, Small Wins & the Goal-Setting Philosophy

**Theme:** Small Shifts, Lasting Change
**Phase:** Foundation

**Summary:** The habit loop (cue → routine → reward). Environment design and friction management. Habit stacking and "if-then" planning. Introduce the Reticular Activating System (RAS) — what you focus on, you find. Emphasise that early goals should be absurdly small to guarantee success. Success breeds belief; belief sustains effort.

**This session includes the compressed goal-setting teaching block (15–20 minutes within the 40-minute learning module).** The placement is deliberate: participants have now had two weeks to settle in, explore their motivations (Week 2), and try a micro-win experiment. They have enough experience to understand what the goal-setting philosophy is trying to solve — and enough self-awareness to apply it honestly.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy — Compressed Teaching Block:**

The facilitator introduces the Infinite/Finite distinction, the limitations of SMART, and the Goal-Setting Cascade in a focused 15–20 minute segment. This is woven into the habits content, not delivered as a separate lecture.

**Facilitator language for the compressed block:**

> *"Before we go deeper into habits, let's talk about what makes a goal worth building a habit around.*
>
> *There are two kinds of goals. A health goal is like a compass direction — 'become someone who moves consistently.' There's no finish line. You express it through rituals and rhythms. It's sustained by your values and your identity. It's resilient — if you have a bad week, the direction hasn't changed.*
>
> *A performance goal is like a destination — 'run 5K in under 30 minutes by June.' It has a clear endpoint. It's measurable. It's sustained by motivation and accountability. But it's also fragile — miss the target, and it can feel like the whole thing has failed.*
>
> *Most of you will hold both. The infinite goal is your compass. The finite goals are your steps. The compass stays steady. The steps adapt.*
>
> *You've probably met SMART goal-setting before. It's a good tool — but it's a shaping tool, not a thinking tool. If you use it before you've done the thinking, you end up with a very well-formatted goal that might not be yours. We're going to do the thinking first.*
>
> *Here's the sequence we'll use throughout this program:*
> *1. What game are you playing — infinite or finite?*
> *2. What's underneath it — what do you want, what do you really want, what are you prepared to give up?*
> *3. Shape it — SMART if it's finite, rituals and rhythms if it's infinite*
> *4. Protect your autonomy — this is your goal, not mine, not someone else's*
> *5. Revisit when new information arrives — every new module is an invitation, not a command*
>
> *And here's the most important thing: a failed tactic isn't a failed strategy. If you miss your three walks this week, that doesn't mean 'become someone who moves' is broken. It means that particular plan didn't survive contact with that particular week. Pick a new tactic. The strategy holds."*

**Foundational Principles Development:** This week connects directly to the third coaching question: *What am I prepared to give up?* Habit change always involves a trade — giving up the comfort of the old routine for the uncertainty of the new one. Environment design is really about making the cost of change lower.

The first principles question — *"What are we here to do?"* — appears here as a practical tool. When a habit attempt fails, rather than spiralling into self-criticism, the participant can ask: "What was I actually trying to achieve? What got in the way? What's the smallest version of this I can still do?" This is the "Do What You Can" philosophy in action, grounded in first principles thinking.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Implicit modelling continues:** The breakout prompt asks participants to notice which habits are already shaping their results. The partner question — "Is the micro-win you've been trying an infinite practice or a finite target? What's really underneath it?" — is a Deepen move using the cascade's language. The instruction to continue or refine the micro-win is a Move. The facilitator can note: *"Notice what just happened in that conversation. One of you opened a topic, the other went deeper, and you arrived at a next step. We're going to name that rhythm soon."*

**Key Content:** Habit loop · Environment design · Habit stacking · RAS awareness · "Too small to fail" goal design · Fogg (Tiny Habits — "after I [anchor], I will [tiny behaviour]") · **Infinite vs. Finite goals** · **SMART limitations and repositioning** · **The Goal-Setting Cascade (5 steps)** · **"A failed tactic isn't a failed strategy"**

**Breakout:** "Which small habits are already shaping my results — for better or worse?" Encourage participants to notice what they'd need to give up for the change they want. After the habits discussion, one partner asks the other: *"Is the micro-win you've been trying an infinite practice or a finite target? What's really underneath it?"* — first live use of the cascade's Steps 1 and 2.

**Home Task:** Watch James Clear on habit stacking (5 min). Try your micro-win for a second week. Notice what helps or hinders. Journal: "What did my habit experiment cost me? What did it give me? Is my micro-win serving an infinite direction I care about — and if so, what would I call that direction?"

**Coaching Skill:** Reflective listening and summarising

**Goal Action:** Continue or refine micro-win. Record what you notice. Participants are now equipped to identify whether their micro-win is a finite tactic serving an infinite direction — but there is no pressure to formalise anything yet. The cascade's Step 3 (Shape It) is deliberately held back until Week 5. Facilitator language: *"You've got the thinking tools now. Let them work on you this week. We'll shape goals more formally soon."*

**Sources:** Clear (Atomic Habits) · Wood (Good Habits, Bad Habits) · Fogg (Tiny Habits) · Huberman (RAS) · Sinek (The Infinite Game — conceptual foundation for Infinite/Finite distinction)

### Week 4 — Stages of Change

**Theme:** Meeting Yourself Where You Are
**Phase:** Foundation

**Summary:** The Transtheoretical Model (pre-contemplation → maintenance). Recognising ambivalence without judgement. Normalise that change is not linear — cycling through stages is expected. Introduce scaling questions: "On a scale of 1–10, how ready do I feel?" Connect to the adaptive goal philosophy: goals should match your current stage, not someone else's.

**Foundational Principles Development:** The Three Prerequisites are revisited here with more precision. The stages of change map directly onto them: pre-contemplation means the dissatisfaction hasn't landed yet; contemplation means the vision is forming but willingness is uncertain; preparation means all three are present and the person is getting ready to act. This gives participants a diagnostic tool — *which prerequisite is missing or weak right now?* — rather than just a label for where they are.

The first principles question helps with ambivalence. When someone is stuck between stages, *"What are we here to do?"* cuts through the internal debate and refocuses on what actually matters.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Implicit modelling, final week before naming:** The scaling question exercise in the breakout is a complete Open → Deepen → Move sequence: Open with "Where am I in my change process?", Deepen with "What would move me from a 5 to a 6?", Move with "What version of my goal can I actually do this week?" The facilitator can foreshadow: *"Next week, we're going to name the pattern you've been practising in these conversations — and give you a simple framework to make it intentional."*

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** The Transtheoretical Model serves as the readiness filter in the Goal-Setting Cascade's Step 3 — the honest question: *"Is this the version of this goal I can actually do this week?"* Participants now understand that their stage of change should determine the size of their finite goal. Someone in contemplation doesn't need a SMART goal — they need a clearer vision or a stronger sense of willingness. Someone in preparation is ready to shape. Someone in action is ready to commit. This is why right-sizing matters.

The facilitator connects stages of change directly to the cascade: *"The cascade's first two steps — what game, and what's underneath — are contemplation work. Step 3 — shaping the goal — is preparation. Actually doing it is action. And cycling back through when life disrupts? That's not failure. That's maintenance."*

**Key Content:** Transtheoretical Model · Ambivalence as data · Scaling questions · Stage-appropriate goals · Three Prerequisites mapped to Stages of Change · **Transtheoretical Model as the cascade's readiness filter** · **Right-sizing goals to current stage**

**Breakout:** "Where am I in my change process for one specific goal?" — triads practise scaling questions. Invite one partner to gently ask: "What would move you from a 5 to a 6?" After the scaling exercise, ask: *"Given your current stage, what version of your goal can you actually do this week? Not your ideal version — your honest version."*

**Home Task:** Identify one area you'd like to improve. Mark your current stage honestly. Journal: "What helps me move from intention to action? Which of the three prerequisites feels weakest for me right now? Is there a gap between the goal I think I should have and the one that matches where I actually am?"

**Coaching Skill:** Meeting people where they are — asking scaling questions

**Goal Action:** Goals remain small. Notice which micro-wins stuck and which didn't — no judgement, just data. Participants may now begin to articulate their infinite direction informally: *"What's the compass heading that these micro-wins are pointing toward?"* No formal commitment required. Facilitator language: *"Curiosity over criticism — this is data, not failure."*

**Sources:** Prochaska & DiClemente · ISSA · ICF competencies

### Week 5 — Foundations of Coaching Conversation

**Theme:** Listening, Curiosity, and Partnership
**Phase:** Foundation

**Summary:** ICF Core Competencies 1–4: Ethics, Coaching Mindset, Agreements, Trust & Safety. Three levels of listening (internal → focused → global). Introduction to powerful questions — open, curious, non-leading. Teach that silence is a coaching skill. Begin to equip participants to support each other with real skill, not just good intentions.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Formally Introduced:** This is the session where the coaching flow is explicitly named and taught. The facilitator reveals that participants have been practising it implicitly for four weeks:

> *"For the past four weeks, every breakout conversation you've had has followed a pattern — even if you didn't notice it. Someone opened a topic. Someone went deeper with a question. And you arrived at a next step.*
>
> *That pattern has a name: Open → Deepen → Move. Three moves. That's it.*
>
> *Open: create space. Let the person talk. Find out what's alive for them. 'What's on your mind?' is a great way in — and credit to Michael Bungay Stanier for that one.*
>
> *Deepen: go beneath the surface. This is where the Three Coaching Questions live — 'What do you want? What do you really want? What are you prepared to give up?' And when the conversation drifts, the first principles question cuts through: 'What are we actually trying to achieve here?' Stay curious a little longer. Rush to advice a little slower.*
>
> *Move: help the person find one honest step. Not your step — theirs. 'What's the version of this you can actually do this week?'*
>
> *The tools you already have — the coaching questions, the first principles question, the goal-setting cascade — power each of these moves. You don't need to learn a new system. You need to recognise that the system you've been absorbing since Week 1 is already a coaching conversation when you arrange it in sequence."*

**Foundational Principles Development:** This is the week where participants learn to use the foundational principles *on each other* through the Open → Deepen → Move flow. The Three Coaching Questions become peer-coaching tools within the Deepen move:

- "What are you hoping for?" (What do you want?)
- "What's really driving that?" (What do you *really* want?)
- "What would that require you to change?" (What are you prepared to give up?)

The first principles question — *"What are we here to do?"* — becomes the most powerful coaching intervention a peer can offer. When a partner is lost in a story, a complaint, or a spiral of self-criticism, the simple redirect of "What are you actually trying to achieve here?" can shift the entire conversation.

Participants practise the full flow in triads for the first time. The emphasis is on asking the question and then *waiting* — holding space for the answer rather than rushing to fill the silence. Bungay Stanier's "And what else?" is taught as the single most useful question for the Deepen move — it prevents the most common peer coaching mistake of latching onto the first thing someone says and trying to fix it.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy — First Goal-Refinement Workshop:** This is where Step 3 of the cascade (Shape It) is practised for the first time, using the Open → Deepen → Move flow as the conversational structure. Participants have had four weeks of micro-win experiments and two weeks since learning the Infinite/Finite distinction and the cascade. Now they formalise one goal.

The workshop format (within the breakout time):

1. **Open:** One partner shares their goal area. The other listens. "What's been on your mind about your goals?"

2. **Deepen:** Partners walk each other through the cascade's thinking steps. **Name your infinite direction.** What's the compass heading? Express it as an identity statement or a rhythm: *"I'm becoming someone who..."* or *"I move my body regularly because..."* **What's underneath it?** "What do you really want from this? What would you need to give up?"

3. **Move:** **Choose one finite tactic.** What's one specific, right-sized step that serves that direction? Apply SMART now — as Step 3, after the thinking is done. **Add one "supportive habit" goal.** A non-movement goal that supports the broader direction — sleep, hydration, a moment of reflection. Still small. Peer-check: *"Is this right-sized for where you are? Does it feel like yours?"*

Facilitator language: *"SMART is step three, not step one. You've done the thinking. Now let's shape what you've found into something you can actually do this week."*

**Key Content:** ICF Competencies 1–4 · Three levels of listening · Open vs. closed questions · The power of silence · **Open → Deepen → Move formally introduced** · Foundational questions as coaching tools · Bungay Stanier (The Coaching Habit — "What's on your mind?", "And what else?", "Stay curious") · Wiseman (Multipliers — amplifying others) · Covey ("Seek first to understand") · **First Goal-Refinement Workshop** · **Goal-Setting Cascade Step 3 practised** · **SMART applied — as step three**

**Breakout:** First half: Triads practise the Open → Deepen → Move flow using the foundational questions. Coach / Coachee / Observer — the observer's role is to notice which move the coach is in and whether they stayed curious long enough in Deepen before moving to Move. Second half: **Goal-Refinement Workshop** — partners walk each other through the cascade using the coaching flow to formalise one goal. Debrief: "What felt natural? Where did curiosity open a door? Did SMART feel different when you'd already done the thinking?"

**Home Task:** Practise Level 2 listening with a friend or partner for 10 minutes (listen without advising). Try using Open → Deepen → Move in any everyday conversation — not as a technique, but as a posture. Journal: "What did I notice when I just listened? What happened when I asked 'what' instead of giving advice? Write down your infinite direction and your first finite tactic — how do they feel?"

**Coaching Skill:** Presence and deep listening — active listening lab. **Open → Deepen → Move as the peer coaching framework.** Introduction of the foundational questions and the goal-setting cascade as coaching tools.

**Goal Action:** First Goal-Refinement Workshop: formalise one SMART goal from your micro-wins — with SMART applied as Step 3 of the cascade, after identifying the game (Step 1) and what's underneath (Step 2). Still small, still flexible. Add one "supportive habit" goal alongside movement. Facilitator language: *"Your health goal is your compass. Your SMART goal is your next few steps. The compass stays steady. The steps adapt."*

**Sources:** ICF Core Competencies · Bungay Stanier (The Coaching Habit) · Brown (Dare to Lead) · Wiseman (Multipliers) · Covey (7 Habits)

### Week 6 — Communication & Emotional Literacy

**Theme:** Conversations That Build Connection
**Phase:** Communication

**Summary:** Non-violent communication: observation vs. judgement. Emotional granularity — the value of naming emotions accurately (Barrett). Brené Brown's "rumble" framework for difficult conversations. This session equips participants with tools that transform relationships at home and at work.

**Foundational Principles in Practice:** Emotional literacy deepens the second coaching question — *What do I really want?* Often what people really want is buried under unexamined emotions. The NVC framework teaches participants to notice the feeling behind the reaction, and the need behind the feeling. This is first principles thinking applied to emotional life: stripping away the story to find the actual need.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Enriched:** This week adds emotional vocabulary to the Deepen move. Before Week 6, participants could ask "What do you really want?" — now they can also notice when someone is naming a thought but not a feeling, and gently invite the feeling: "That sounds frustrating — is that what's underneath?" Barrett's emotional granularity means Deepen becomes more precise. The coaching flow's three moves don't change — what changes is the quality of attention participants bring to each one.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** This is a consolidation week for goals — no new goal content is introduced. The focus is on deepening self-awareness through emotional literacy, which will improve the quality of goal-setting going forward. When participants can name what they feel, they get better at answering "What do I *really* want?"

**Key Content:** NVC principles · Emotional granularity (Barrett) · Rumbling with vulnerability (Brown)

**Breakout:** "How do emotions influence my daily choices?" — practise labelling emotions and NVC observations in pairs. Use the Open → Deepen → Move flow, with particular attention to Deepen: "What are you actually feeling right now — not what you think you should feel?"

**Home Task:** Watch Marc Brackett on emotion naming (10 min). Journal: "What conversation have I been avoiding, and why? What do I *really* need from that conversation?" Try using Open → Deepen → Move in one conversation this week and notice what happens.

**Coaching Skill:** Empathic reflection — naming emotions safely

**Goal Action:** No new goals. Consolidate existing micro-wins and the Week 5 formal goal. Peer pod check-in using the cascade: *"Is your tactic still serving your direction? Does it need adjusting?"* Facilitator language: *"Adjusting your goal means you're paying attention."*

### Week 7 — Movement Foundations

**Theme:** Doing What You Can — Physically
**Phase:** The Body

**Summary:** Functional movement patterns: squat, hinge, push, pull, carry, core. Movement quality, posture, and mobility as foundations for everything else. The evolutionary context — our bodies evolved for regular, varied movement (Lieberman). Minimum effective dose: you don't need extreme workouts, you need regular, appropriate, repeatable ones. Explicit adaptation guidance for participants with chronic conditions, injuries, or mobility limitations.

**Foundational Principles in Practice:** The first principles question is particularly sharp in the physical domain. When participants carry old stories about exercise — "I'm not athletic," "I hate the gym," "I tried before and failed" — the question *"What are we here to do?"* cuts through the narrative. We're not here to become athletes. We're here to move well enough to live the life we want. That reframe changes everything.

The third coaching question — *What am I prepared to give up?* — surfaces here as participants negotiate the real cost of adding movement: time, comfort, the identity of "someone who doesn't exercise."

**Open → Deepen → Move — Applied to Physical Goals:** The coaching flow now operates in a new domain. In the breakout, the Open move invites participants to explore their relationship with movement — not their exercise plan, but their actual relationship with physical activity. The Deepen move uses the foundational questions to get underneath old stories and beliefs about the body. The Move step draws on the cascade to find a right-sized physical step. Facilitator language: *"Use the same three moves you've been practising. Open into the story. Deepen into what's really there. Move toward what's actually possible."*

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** Movement is where many participants first encounter the tension between infinite and finite goals. The infinite direction might be "become someone who moves regularly." The finite tactic might be "try three squat patterns this week." The facilitator explicitly names this: *"Notice whether you're drawn to a compass heading or a destination. Both are fine. Just know which one you're holding."*

New information from the movement module is an invitation to revisit existing goals (Cascade Step 5). Some participants may want to add a movement goal; others may realise their existing goal already covers movement. The instruction is: *"Every new lesson is an invitation, not a command. Add gently, not greedily."*

**Key Content:** Six movement patterns · Mobility & posture basics · Evolutionary context for movement · Minimum effective dose · "Do what you can" applied to exercise · Adaptation and modification guidance · McGonigal (movement as joy, not obligation)

**Breakout:** "How does my relationship with movement affect the rest of my life?" — link physical to emotional and professional. Use Open → Deepen → Move to explore. Encourage participants to use the foundational questions with their partners.

**Home Task:** 20-min movement check-in video (mobility + breath) or Physionic video on movement & health — participant choice. Journal: "How can I fit movement into my current life rhythm? What story about exercise do I need to set aside?"

**Coaching Skill:** Goal-setting language — turning intention into action

**Goal Action:** Participants may add a movement-related micro-goal if it feels right — using the cascade: What game? What's underneath? Shape it. Is it right-sized? No pressure. Facilitator language: *"Add gently, not greedily. One new tactic that serves your direction is better than five that overwhelm you."*

### Week 8 — Strength Training for Life

**Theme:** The Body Learns Through Challenge
**Phase:** The Body

**Summary:** Why resistance training matters for everyone: muscle mass, bone density, metabolic health, insulin sensitivity, confidence, longevity. Progressive overload and the adaptation cycle (stimulus → recovery → growth). Distinguish patterns from exercises. Myths about strength and ageing. Programming basics — sets, reps, intensity — kept simple. Connect physical resilience to psychological resilience: "Training the body trains the mind to recover."

**Foundational Principles in Practice:** Strength training is a physical embodiment of first principles thinking. You strip away the noise — the perfect gym, the perfect routine, the perfect body — and focus on what's actually in front of you: can I do one more rep than last time? Can I lift something I couldn't lift before? That's progress. The question *"What are we here to do?"* applied to strength work is beautifully simple: get a little stronger than yesterday, in whatever way is available to you today.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Applied to Strength Beliefs:** The breakout prompt ("How do my beliefs about strength influence how I show up in other areas?") is a natural Open → Deepen → Move sequence. Open: share a belief about strength. Deepen: where did that belief come from? Is it still serving you? Move: what would shift if that belief changed? The coaching flow applied to beliefs about the body is particularly powerful because it connects physical capability to psychological identity.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** Progressive overload is the fitness equivalent of the goal-setting cascade in action. The infinite direction is "get stronger." Each session's finite tactic is "add a rep, add a kilo, improve the form." The progressive overload cycle *is* the Observe → Reflect → Adjust → Recommit loop applied to a barbell. The facilitator can name this connection: *"What you're doing in the gym is what you're learning to do in life — assess where you are, make the smallest meaningful upgrade, and show up again."*

**Mid-course goal check:** This is the first of two formal **Revision Checkpoints** built into the program. Protected time (10–15 minutes) within the session for participants to review all current goals against new knowledge. The cascade's Step 5 (Revisit) is the framework, and the Open → Deepen → Move flow structures the peer conversation around it:

- **Open:** "How are your goals feeling right now?"
- **Deepen:** "Does your finite tactic still serve your infinite direction? Has new information changed what's right-sized for you?"
- **Move:** "Do you want to add, adjust, or release anything? What's the smallest upgrade that feels right — not ideal, just right for this week?"

Facilitator language: *"Now that you understand recovery and progressive overload, does your current plan still make sense? What small upgrade feels right — not ideal, not ambitious, just right for this week?"*

**Key Content:** Benefits of resistance training · Progressive overload & adaptation · Movement patterns vs. exercises · Minimum effective dose · Strength as independence · Adaptation options · Wiseman (Multipliers — how peers amplify each other's confidence) · **First Revision Checkpoint**

**Breakout:** "How do my beliefs about strength influence how I show up in other areas — work, relationships, confidence?" Use Open → Deepen → Move to explore. Include Revision Checkpoint peer coaching.

**Home Task:** Movement Snapshot: 3 sets each of squat, push-up, hinge, row, plank (or adapted versions). Optional: Physionic video on resistance training benefits. Journal: "How did my body feel? Which pattern felt easiest/most limited?"

**Coaching Skill:** Reframing & empowerment — shifting "I can't" to "Here's what I can do." Introduction to tactical empathy skills (mirroring, labelling) in coaching practice.

**Goal Action:** **Revision Checkpoint 1:** Review all current goals in light of Weeks 7–8 content. Finite tactics may be revised, replaced, or released. ### Week 9 — Aerobic Fitness & Longevity

**Theme:** Building the Engine
**Phase:** The Body

**Summary:** VO₂ max and its link to longevity and quality of life. Aerobic vs. anaerobic systems and mitochondrial health. Introduction to self-regulated aerobic training, using the Maffetone Method (MAF heart-rate training, 180-Age formula) as one practical and conservative framework. Acknowledge that individualised HR zones may be more accurate — the MAF approach is a useful starting heuristic, not the only method. Emphasise that early aerobic work may mean mostly walking — that's expected and correct.

**Foundational Principles in Practice:** "Do What You Can" meets cardio. For many participants, the gap between what they think aerobic fitness should look like (running, spinning, breathless effort) and what it actually requires at the start (walking) creates cognitive dissonance. The first principles question resolves it: *"What are we here to do?"* Build a base of aerobic health. Walking does that. The story about what "real exercise" looks like is just a story.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Applied to the "Walking Isn't Real Exercise" Story:** The coaching flow is particularly valuable this week for participants experiencing cognitive dissonance. A peer coach using the flow might: Open with "How do you feel about the idea that walking might be your primary training for a while?" Deepen with "What story are you telling yourself about what 'real exercise' is? Where did that come from?" Move with "What version of aerobic training actually fits your life this week?" The flow helps participants process the gap between expectation and reality without judgment.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** Aerobic training is where the Infinite/Finite distinction proves its worth most tangibly. The infinite direction: "build a body that moves well for decades." The finite tactic: "walk for 30 minutes three times this week at a heart rate below 140." When the finite tactic feels embarrassingly small (walking instead of running), the infinite frame protects it: *"This tactic serves a strategy that outlasts any single workout."*

Participants who have been chasing finite performance goals may recognise for the first time that their real aspiration is infinite — and that changes how they evaluate progress. Facilitator language: *"If your compass heading is 'stay capable and independent for decades,' then walking at a low heart rate isn't a compromise. It's the strategy."*

**Coaching Skill:** Reframing obstacles — from excuses to feedback

Finite: walk 3× this week.) What's underneath? Shape it. Is it right-sized? Adapt existing goals with new knowledge. Facilitator language: *"Success expands capacity — let it grow you slowly."*

### Week 10 — Recovery, Sleep & Stress

**Theme:** The Power of Rest
**Phase:** Fuel & Recovery

**Summary:** Stress-recovery balance and the adaptation cycle applied to life, not just training. Sleep hygiene, circadian rhythms, and practical protocols. HRV as a window into recovery status — with scope-and-limitations framing. Psychological resilience and self-compassion — "How do I rest without quitting?" Redefine discipline as self-respect, not punishment.

**Foundational Principles in Practice:** Recovery surfaces a deep version of the third coaching question: *What am I prepared to give up?* For many participants, what they need to give up is the belief that rest is laziness. Covey's "Sharpen the Saw" reframes recovery as essential investment. The first principles question applied to rest: *"What are we here to do?"* — sustain yourself for the long haul, not burn bright and burn out.

Siegel's Window of Tolerance is introduced here as a framework for understanding how stress narrows capacity. This plants a seed that will be revisited powerfully in Week 11.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Applied to Rest and Resistance:** The coaching flow this week may encounter resistance — participants who intellectually accept the value of rest but emotionally resist it. The Deepen move is critical: "You know rest matters — so what's actually stopping you? What belief about productivity or self-worth would you have to release?" This is where the coaching flow does its most counter-cultural work, because it helps participants sit with the discomfort of changing a deeply held belief rather than rushing to a tactic.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** Recovery is where the infinite frame does its most counter-cultural work. Participants steeped in achievement culture may resist the idea that rest is part of the strategy. The facilitator explicitly connects rest to the cascade: *"Your infinite direction includes being here next month, and next year. Rest isn't the absence of progress — it's what makes progress sustainable. If your tactics don't include recovery, they're working against your strategy."*

This is also where participants may need to release a finite goal that's become counterproductive. A sleep-deprived participant pushing for four workouts a week may need to hear: *"That tactic is no longer serving your direction. What would? Maybe it's three workouts and one early bedtime."* Releasing a tactic without shame is the cascade's built-in safety valve.

**Key Content:** Stress-recovery balance · Sleep hygiene basics · HRV overview (scope & limitations) · Circadian anchoring · Rest as performance strategy · Siegel (Window of Tolerance) · Covey ("Sharpen the Saw")

**Breakout:** "What is my relationship with rest? Do I let myself recover, or do I push through?" — use Open → Deepen → Move to normalise the struggle.

**Home Task:** Sleep log for three nights (bed time, wake time, quality rating). Optional: Physionic video on sleep and recovery. Journal: "What does true rest look like for me?"

**Coaching Skill:** Holding space and normalising setbacks

**Goal Action:** Review all current goals through the lens of sustainability. Are they still appropriate? Scale up, down, or sideways — no shame. Facilitator language: *"Is this the version of this goal you can actually sustain — not just do once, but keep doing? If not, right-size it. Adjusting your goal means you're paying attention."*

### Week 11 — When the Wheels Come Off

**Theme:** Setbacks, Compassion & Getting Back
**Phase:** Fuel & Recovery

**Summary:** A dedicated session on setbacks, relapse, and failure — placed where participants are deep enough to have actually struggled. Normalise the dip: setbacks are part of every change process, not evidence of failure. Introduce the Window of Tolerance (Siegel) to explain why stress narrows capacity — it's neurobiological, not weakness. Teach self-compassion as a practised skill using Neff's three-component model (self-kindness, common humanity, mindfulness). Complete the stress cycle (Nagoski) before seeking the lesson. Reframe adversity as data (Holiday) — but only after compassion.

**Foundational Principles — Full Return:** This is the session where the foundational principles do their deepest work. When participants have stalled, relapsed, or lost momentum, the Three Prerequisites are revisited with fresh eyes:

- *Is my dissatisfaction still real?* — Sometimes a goal stalls because the original dissatisfaction has shifted. That's not failure; it's information.
- *Is my vision still clear?* — Sometimes the vision was never truly the participant's own. It was someone else's expectation, a cultural standard, or an outdated version of what they thought they should want.
- *Am I still willing? And what would willingness look like from where I am right now?* — Not from where I was. Not from where I wish I was. From here.

The first principles question is at its most powerful: *"What are we here to do?"* When everything else has been stripped away — the excuses, the self-criticism, the comparison with others, the disappointment — what remains is the actual goal. And from that clarity, the smallest next step becomes visible.

The facilitator connects this explicitly to the "Do What You Can" philosophy: *"When the plan breaks — and it will — the question isn't 'how do I get back to where I was?' It's 'what can I do right now, from where I am, with what I've got?'"*

The teaching sequence matters: **compassion first, insight second.** Self-compassion (Neff) → complete the stress cycle (Nagoski) → then, and only then, look for the lesson in the obstacle (Holiday). Reversing this order feels dismissive.

**Open → Deepen → Move — At Its Most Important:** This is the session where the coaching flow does its heaviest emotional lifting. The Deepen move must resist the urge to fix, reframe, or silver-line. In the breakout, peers practise being with someone in a setback — not pulling them out of it. The Open move creates space for the story to be told honestly. The Deepen move holds space for the emotion underneath: "What are you telling yourself about this? What would you say to a friend?" The Move step comes only after compassion has landed — and it may be very small: "What's the gentlest thing you could do for yourself this week?"

The observer in the triad has a specific role this week: notice whether the coach rushed from Deepen to Move. The most common mistake in setback conversations is solving too quickly. "Stay curious a little longer" is the discipline that matters most here.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy — The Setback Reframe in Full:**

This is the session where "A failed tactic isn't a failed strategy" does its heaviest lifting. The facilitator draws the distinction explicitly:

> *"Some of you have missed goals over the past few weeks. Some of you have quietly abandoned them. Here's what I want you to hear: the tactic failed. The strategy didn't.*
>
> *Your compass heading — 'become someone who moves,' 'nourish myself well,' 'build a life that feels strong' — that hasn't changed. What happened is that a particular plan didn't survive contact with a particular set of circumstances.*
>
> *That's not failure. That's data.*
>
> *The question now is: what's the next tactic? What version of your goal matches where you actually are — not where you were, not where you wish you were — right now?"*

The Goal-Setting Cascade's Step 5 (Revisit) is the formal framework for recovery:
1. Revisit Step 1: Is this still the right game? (Maybe the goal type needs to shift — from finite back to infinite, or from one domain to another.)
2. Revisit Step 2: What's underneath the stall? (Was the goal genuinely mine? Was the cost too high? Did circumstances change?)
3. Revisit Step 3: What's the right-sized next tactic? (Smaller, different, or — sometimes — released entirely.)

**Key Content:** Normalising setbacks · Window of Tolerance · Self-compassion (Neff: self-kindness, common humanity, mindfulness) · Completing the stress cycle (Nagoski) · The obstacle as data (Holiday) · **Three Prerequisites revisited** · **First Principles: "What are we here to do?" — applied to recovery** · **"A failed tactic isn't a failed strategy" — the full reframe** · **Goal-Setting Cascade applied to setbacks** · **Open → Deepen → Move applied to compassionate inquiry** · "Do What You Can" reconnection

**Breakout:** Compassionate Inquiry Triads using the full Open → Deepen → Move flow. **Open:** "Share a time you fell off track with something that mattered to you. What happened?" **Deepen:** "What did you tell yourself about it? What would you say to a friend?" Coaches practise being with someone in it — not pulling them out. **Move** (only if it feels right): "What's the smallest thing that might help right now?" Observer watches for the rush from Deepen to Move.

**Home Task:** Self-compassion writing exercise (Neff): address yourself through three lenses — self-kindness, common humanity, mindfulness. Journal: "What do I say to myself when I fail? What would I say to a friend? What changes if I close that gap?"

**Coaching Skill:** Compassionate inquiry — exploring setbacks with curiosity and warmth, without rushing to fix, minimise, or reframe. The Deepen move at its most demanding.

**Goal Action:** Revisit any stalled or abandoned goals using the cascade. Three options: **recommit** (same infinite direction, new finite tactic), **adjust** (modify the goal to fit current reality — right-size it), or **release** (let this one go, without shame). Facilitator language: *"Releasing a goal is not failure. Sometimes a goal served its purpose by teaching you what you don't actually want. That's data. The strategy hasn't changed. Only the tactic needs adjusting."*

**Sources:** Neff (Self-Compassion) · Brown (Dare to Lead, Atlas of the Heart) · Prochaska & DiClemente · ISSA Transformation Specialist · Holiday (The Obstacle Is the Way) · Nagoski & Nagoski (Burnout) · Siegel (Window of Tolerance) · Bungay Stanier (The Coaching Habit — "stay curious" applied to compassionate inquiry)

### Week 12 — Nutrition I: Foundations of Fuelling

**Theme:** Understanding What We Eat
**Phase:** Fuel & Recovery

**Summary:** Macronutrients, micronutrients, hydration, fibre. Energy balance, hunger cues, and metabolic flexibility. The evolutionary mismatch — our bodies evolved for a nutritional environment very different from the one we live in (Lieberman). This is an education session, not a prescription. No specific diet is recommended. Gentle screening/referral language for participants with eating disorder history — scope of practice matters.

**Foundational Principles in Practice:** Nutrition is deeply personal territory, and the coaching questions help participants navigate it honestly. *What do I want?* (to feel better, to have more energy, to fit my clothes). *What do I really want?* (to feel at peace with food, to stop the cycle of restriction and guilt, to nourish myself without obsession). *What am I prepared to give up?* (convenience, the comfort of habitual eating, the fantasy of a quick fix).

The first principles question cuts through diet culture noise: *"What are we here to do?"* Nourish ourselves well enough to live the life we want. Not achieve a number. Not follow a plan. Nourish ourselves.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Applied to Food with Care:** Nutrition conversations require particular sensitivity in the Deepen move. Participants may carry shame, history with disordered eating, or strong tribal identities around food. The Open move needs to be genuinely non-judgemental. The Deepen move should explore curiosity about food patterns without prescribing. The Move step this week is deliberately observation-based: no new goals, just notice. Facilitator language: *"Use your coaching skills gently here. Open the conversation. Deepen with curiosity, not judgment. And the Move this week is simply to observe — no changes, just awareness."*

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** Nutrition is where the Infinite/Finite distinction protects participants from the most common trap in wellness: mistaking a finite diet for an infinite practice. The facilitator names it: *"'Lose 10 pounds by March' is a finite goal. 'Become someone who eats well' is infinite. Both are legitimate — but know which one you're holding. The finite one will end. The infinite one won't. And if the finite one doesn't serve the infinite, it's the wrong tactic."*

No nutrition goals are imposed. This is education, not prescription. If participants want to set a nutrition-related goal, the instruction is: *"Observe first. We have three weeks of nutrition content. Let the information land before you commit to a tactic."*

**Key Content:** Macronutrients & micronutrients · Energy balance · Hunger cues · Metabolic flexibility · Evolutionary mismatch · Scope-of-practice safeguards · Taylor (metabolic health evidence)

**Breakout:** "How does food affect my mood and energy?" — non-judgemental exploration using Open → Deepen → Move. Encourage participants to use curiosity rather than prescription.

**Home Task:** Watch Gil Carvalho (Nutrition Made Simple) on reading nutrition research or debunking a common myth (~10 min). Food awareness log for 3 days (no judgement, just observation). Journal: "How does food affect my mood and energy?"

**Coaching Skill:** Using neutral language and curiosity around sensitive topics

**Goal Action:** No new goals imposed. Observe. Facilitator language: *"Every new lesson is an invitation, not a command. Let this one settle before you act on it."*

**Sources:** ISSA Nutrition · Lieberman (Story of the Human Body) · Attia (Outlive) · Taylor (metabolic health, DiRECT trial)

### Week 13 — Nutrition II: Evaluating Dietary Strategies

**Theme:** Thinking Clearly About Food
**Phase:** Fuel & Recovery

**Summary:** Rather than surveying five dietary approaches in 40 minutes, this session teaches a framework for evaluating any dietary claim. Participants learn criteria: what does the evidence actually show? Who funded the research? What's the long-term data? Is the claim too good to be true? Two or three approaches are used as worked examples (e.g., Mediterranean, intermittent fasting). Participants practise applying the framework to a dietary claim they've encountered.

**Foundational Principles in Practice:** First principles thinking is directly applicable to nutrition claims. Strip away the marketing, the influencer endorsements, the tribal identity — *"What does the evidence actually show?"* This is "What are we here to do?" applied to information: we're here to make informed decisions, not to join a diet tribe.

**Open → Deepen → Move — Applied to Critical Thinking:** The coaching flow operates on ideas this week as much as on feelings. The Open move surfaces a dietary belief the participant has held. The Deepen move applies the evaluation framework: "What's the evidence behind that belief? Where did you first hear it? What would change if the evidence didn't support it?" The Move step might be as simple as "What's one assumption about food I'm willing to re-examine this week?" This is the coaching flow applied to information literacy — a skill that transfers far beyond nutrition.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** The evaluation framework this session teaches is the goal-setting cascade applied to information rather than behaviour. Step 1: What kind of claim is this — a lifestyle direction or a specific target? Step 2: What's underneath it — evidence or ideology? Step 3: Does it survive scrutiny? The critical thinking skill transfers directly.

Participants may now be ready to add or adjust a nutrition-related goal. The cascade applies in full: *"If you're drawn to a nutrition change, run it through the cascade first. What game is it? What's underneath your interest? Shape it — right-sized to this week. Make sure it feels like yours, not borrowed from a diet book."*

**Key Content:** Evaluation framework for dietary claims · Evidence-based worked examples · Critical thinking in practice · Carvalho (Nutrition Made Simple) · Guess (glucose metabolism evidence) · Taylor (clinical trial data)

**Breakout:** "What dietary claim have I accepted without really examining it?" — practise the evaluation framework in pairs using Open → Deepen → Move.

**Home Task:** Watch Gil Carvalho evaluating a dietary approach (~10 min). Apply the session's framework to one dietary claim you've encountered. Journal: "What did I discover when I looked at the evidence?"

**Coaching Skill:** Encouraging autonomy and informed experimentation

**Goal Action:** Participants may add or adjust a nutrition-related goal — but only if it's genuinely theirs, not borrowed from a diet book. The cascade is the quality check: *"Run it through. What game? What's underneath? Is it right-sized? Is it yours?"* Facilitator language: *"Your health goal is your compass. If a nutrition tactic serves that compass, add it gently. If it doesn't, leave it."*

**Sources:** ISSA Nutrition · Attia (Outlive) · Taylor · Guess · Carvalho (Nutrition Made Simple)

### Week 14 — Nutrition III: Emotional Relationship with Food

**Theme:** Beyond Macros — The Psychology of Eating
**Phase:** Fuel & Recovery (final session of this phase)

**Summary:** Hunger vs. appetite, emotional eating, and the psychology of cravings. Mindfulness applied to eating. Addressing diet culture with self-compassion. The role of stress, sleep, and social context in food choices. Integrate nutrition into the broader "Whole" framework — food is one dimension of wellbeing, not the whole picture.

**Foundational Principles in Practice:** The second coaching question — *What do I really want?* — is at its most poignant here. When someone reaches for food they don't need, what are they actually reaching for? Comfort? Connection? Relief? The question doesn't judge. It illuminates. And from that illumination, choice becomes possible.

**Open → Deepen → Move — At Its Most Intimate:** This is one of the most emotionally demanding applications of the coaching flow. The Deepen move around food and emotion requires the same discipline as Week 11's setbacks session: stay with the feeling before reaching for a solution. A peer coach might Open with "What's your relationship with food really like — not what you eat, but how you feel about what you eat?" Deepen with "When you reach for food you don't need, what are you actually reaching for?" Move with "What's one gentle thing you could try this week — not a rule, just an experiment?"

The observer's role is again to watch for the rush from Deepen to Move. With food, the temptation to prescribe is even stronger than with setbacks.

**Goal-Setting Philosophy Connection:** This session is where participants encounter the deepest version of Step 2 (What's Underneath It?) applied to food. Emotional eating is a case study in how surface goals ("eat less sugar") can miss the actual need ("manage stress without numbing"). The cascade helps: *"Before you set a rule about food, ask what's underneath the pattern you're trying to change. The rule might be the wrong tactic. The real goal might need a completely different approach — rest, connection, or self-compassion."*

This is also the **second formal Revision Checkpoint** — the mid-course review. Protected time (15–20 minutes) to revisit all goals in light of the full Body and Nutrition sequence.

**Key Content:** Emotional eating patterns · Mindful eating practice · Diet culture awareness · Stress-food connection · Self-compassion around food

**Breakout:** "What drives my food choices beyond nutrition — emotion, culture, habit, convenience?" — deep, non-judgemental exploration using Open → Deepen → Move.

**Home Task:** Mindful-eating exercise: one meal without screens or distractions. Journal: "What's behind my cravings — hunger, habit, or emotion?"

**Coaching Skill:** Compassionate inquiry — exploring without fixing

**Goal Action:** **Revision Checkpoint 2:** Post-nutrition series review. Revisit all goals in light of Weeks 7–14 content using Open → Deepen → Move in peer pairs. The full cascade is the framework:

1. **Open / Step 1 — What game?** For each goal: is it infinite or finite? Has the type shifted? Some finite goals may want to become infinite practices; some infinite aspirations may now be ready for a finite commitment.
2. **Deepen / Step 2 — What's underneath?** Does the motivation still hold? Has deeper understanding changed what you really want?
3. **Deepen / Step 3 — Reshape.** Right-size to current reality. "Does this finite step still serve my infinite direction, given what I now know?"
4. **Deepen / Step 4 — Whose goal is this?** After three weeks of nutrition content, some participants may have absorbed goals from the material rather than from themselves. The autonomy check matters.
5. **Move / Step 5 — What's next?** Connect goals to values. "Which goals feel most like *me*?"

Facilitator language: *"You've learned a lot in the past eight weeks. Some of that learning may have changed what you want. That's not inconsistency — that's growth. Run your goals through the cascade. Keep what serves your direction. Release what doesn't. Add gently, not greedily."*

**Foundational Principles — Integration:** The Three Prerequisites are revisited one final time, now with 15 weeks of depth behind them. Participants can see how far they've come:

**Goal-Setting Philosophy — The Infinite Goal Becomes Explicit:**

## Design Notes & Key Decisions

**Foundational principles as backbone, not decoration:** The Three Prerequisites, Three Coaching Questions, and First Principles question are not mentioned once and forgotten. They are introduced in Week 1, developed through Weeks 2–5, applied to physical and nutritional content in Weeks 7–14, returned to with full force in Week 11 (setbacks), and handed over as permanent self-coaching tools in Weeks 15–16.

**Open → Deepen → Move as the conversational engine:** The coaching flow is the mechanism through which the foundational principles and goal-setting cascade come alive in peer conversation. It is implicitly modelled for four weeks before being formally taught in Week 5, then applied with increasing depth across every subsequent session. The three moves stay the same; what changes is the richness participants bring to each move as new knowledge accumulates. By Week 16, the flow is instinctive — and transferable to any conversation, not just coaching.

**Bungay Stanier as credited influence:** Michael Bungay Stanier's work (The Coaching Habit, The Advice Trap) directly informs the coaching stance — "stay curious a little longer, rush to advice a little slower" — and contributes specific starter questions ("What's on your mind?", "And what else?", "What's the real challenge here for you?"). He is credited as an influence and recommended as further reading. The Open → Deepen → Move structure itself is built on Crawford Coaching's foundational principles; Bungay Stanier teaches the posture, the program provides the architecture.

**Goal-setting as operating system, not event:** The goal-setting philosophy does not occupy its own dedicated session. It is taught in a compressed block (Week 3), practised in a workshop (Week 5), tested against new knowledge (Weeks 7–14), stress-tested by setbacks (Week 11), and handed over as a permanent tool (Weeks 15–16). This threading is what makes it an operating system rather than a one-off exercise.

**SMART is step three, not step one:** This repositioning is consistent throughout. SMART is never introduced without the upstream thinking (Steps 1 and 2 of the cascade) having been done first. Participants learn to do the thinking before the planning.

**"A failed tactic isn't a failed strategy" as the program's recovery mechanism:** This reframe appears in the compressed teaching block (Week 3), is reinforced through weekly check-ins, reaches full force in Week 11 (setbacks), and is handed over as a permanent principle in Week 16. It is the goal-setting philosophy's most important contribution to participant resilience.

**Two formal Revision Checkpoints:** Week 8 (post-Body phase) and Week 14 (post-Nutrition series) provide protected time for participants to run their goals through the cascade with new knowledge. These are not ad hoc — they are built into the session structure. Open → Deepen → Move provides the conversational framework for peer-supported revision.

**Progressive goals:** Goals start absurdly small and evolve with the content. Each new module is an invitation to add or refine a goal, never an obligation. "Success breeds success." The facilitator language guide provides consistent phrasing: "Add gently, not greedily."

**Two-tier content delivery:** Canva slide decks are airy and visual (6–8 content slides per 40-min block). Detailed notes, reflections, and coaching practice go in the accompanying PDF worksheet (2–4 pages).

**Peer coaching as the engine:** Participants learn to coach each other through triads (Coach / Coachee / Observer) using the Open → Deepen → Move flow. Skills build progressively from listening (Weeks 1–4) to conscious practice (Weeks 5–8) to confident application (Weeks 9–12) to autonomous peer coaching (Weeks 13–16). The foundational questions, the goal-setting cascade, and the coaching flow give peer coaches a powerful, simple toolkit.

**PERMA as lens, not scorecard:** PERMA is introduced as a framework for understanding that wellbeing is broader than happiness. The sailboat metaphor (Kaufman) provides an intuitive visual for how PERMA dimensions interact.

**Values throughout:** Core values are established in Week 1 (live card sort) and revisited repeatedly. They anchor goal-setting, decision-making, and identity work across all modules. In Week 15, they become the explicit foundation for the infinite goal's identity statement.

**The power of the pack:** Group dynamics are not incidental — they are the delivery mechanism. Participants gain self-esteem by helping others, which fuels their own progress. Coaching skills — and particularly the Open → Deepen → Move flow — transfer directly to professional and personal relationships.

**Adaptive, not prescriptive:** "Do what you can" means every recommendation flexes to fit real life. Plans adapt under scrutiny and evolve as new information becomes available. Anti–all-or-nothing thinking. The goal-setting cascade's built-in right-sizing mechanism (Step 3) and revision protocol (Step 5) make adaptability structural rather than aspirational.

**First principles in setbacks:** The first principles question — *"What are we here to do?"* — is deliberately positioned as the program's most powerful recovery tool. When everything else falls away, this question remains.

**Open → Deepen → Move beyond the program:** The coaching flow is explicitly framed as transferable. It works in peer coaching triads, but it also works in a conversation with a partner, a colleague, or a teenager. And the self-coaching version — Observe → Reflect → Adjust → Recommit — means participants can run the flow on themselves for the rest of their lives. This is the program's most portable takeaway.

## Accessibility & Adaptation Statement

Whole is designed to meet participants where they are — physically, emotionally, and practically. The "Do What You Can" philosophy applies to every recommendation in the program, including goal-setting and peer coaching. Participants with disabilities, chronic conditions, or significant movement limitations are welcome and supported. All physical content (Weeks 7–9 in particular) includes adaptation guidance and alternative approaches. The goal-setting cascade's right-sizing mechanism (Step 3) and revision protocol (Step 5) ensure that goals always match current capacity, not idealised capacity. The Open → Deepen → Move coaching flow adapts naturally to any participant's communication style and comfort level. If a participant's needs exceed the facilitator's scope, referral to an appropriate professional is always the responsible choice. The facilitator is not a therapist or medical practitioner — and will say so clearly when needed.
